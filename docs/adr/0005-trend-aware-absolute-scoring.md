# Trend-aware absolute scoring (supersedes the legacy P/T/R formula)

The end-of-game score is a single function: `compute_score(snapshots,
starting_cash)` in `world/scoring.py`. It is **absolute** — score in
`[0, 100]` derived only from the play history and the starting cash
endowment — and **trend-aware** — each of treasury, population, and
happiness contributes a level / trend / trough triple, joined with a
renewable-share term and a solvency term.

A second formula used to live alongside it. `world/scoring.py` (the
legacy module, deleted in this commit) computed a **baseline-relative**
score: `(P/P_ref, tanh(T/T_ref), R)` against `baselines/seed_{N}.json`
generated by a scripted-agent run on the same seed. Its output was a
sub-2.0 weighted sum, not a `[0, 100]` headline. The HTTP `/score`
endpoint was switched to the trend-aware formula earlier;
`evaluate.py::_score_breakdown` and the smoke test continued to call
the legacy form, plus a hand-rolled inline copy in `evaluate.py` for
the `--api-url` mode where no live `World` was available.

Three implementations of "scoring" — two of the legacy formula, one of
the new one — was a [shallow module] cluster. The interface was
not "what is the score?" but "which of the three callsites is asking?"

We retire the legacy form rather than keep it alongside, because:

- **Baseline-relative scoring forces a baseline-keeping discipline.**
  Every seed needs a committed `baselines/seed_{N}.json`. Adding a
  scenario or rebalancing economics invalidates every baseline and
  forces a regeneration sweep.
- **The trend-aware formula already encodes the same intent without
  the baseline.** "Did treasury grow?" is captured by the treasury
  trend term; "did population hold up?" by the population trough;
  "did the agent stay solvent?" by the solvency fraction.
- **One scorer = one place to tune.** Scale anchors
  (`TREASURY_SCALE`, `POP_TARGET`, `CAGR_SCALE_*`, `CVAR_ALPHA`) live
  in `world/scoring.py` only. Recalibration is a one-file change.

The retirement is irreversible by design — `baselines/seed_{N}.json`
files stay on disk for byte-determinism regression tests
(`agents/tests/test_scripted_smoke.py` still pins `(P, T)` against the
committed payload), but no scorer reads them.

## What this means for `evaluate.py`

`cmd_eval` now calls `api.score()` against the same `ApiClient` it
already used for play. `/score` reads per-day snapshots off
`recorder.states_path` and runs `compute_score`. The in-process mode
(`_make_inprocess_client`) and the `--api-url` mode go through the
same HTTP interface, so there is no second implementation to keep in
sync. The printed `score` key changes shape from the legacy
nine-field flat dict `{P, P_ref, p_term, T, T_ref, t_term, R, r_term,
score}` to the trend-aware `{n_days, score, components: {...}}`. Any
external parser of the old shape breaks once at this commit.

## Why this is an ADR, not just a refactor

A future contributor running a "benchmark the agent against a scripted
baseline" experiment could reasonably re-suggest the legacy shape —
the name "baseline-relative scoring" sounds like it should exist.
This ADR records that we considered it, picked absolute trend-aware
instead, and that the deletion was load-bearing for keeping scale
anchors in one place. Reopen the question only if the maintainer
wants to take on the baseline-keeping discipline again.

## Reshape: the headline must asymptote to 100

The first cut of `compute_score` defined the per-axis trend as the
tanh-mapped daily CAGR (or linear slope for treasury). That formula
returns 0.5 for any signal with zero growth — including a signal
parked at its utility ceiling. With happiness clipped at
`HAPPINESS_CEIL=1.2`, a player at the cap had `trend_happy = 0.5` no
matter what, capping `axis_happy` at 0.8 and the achievable headline
at roughly 85. A `/100` headline whose top 15 points are mathematically
unreachable is dishonest signage.

The trend definition is now the **better of growth and the recent
trailing window**:

    trend = max(growth_score, mean(u(xs[-window:])))

with `window = max(1, n // 4)`. Growth keeps doing the work while
the signal is still climbing; once it saturates, the recent-window
utility takes over and credits "stayed at the top." A flat-at-zero
signal still scores trend near zero (recent-window mean is low). A
declining run still scores trend low (both terms low). A
sustained-at-max run scores trend near 1, which is what its time
series deserves.

The quarter-window pick is deliberately not the last-10%: a strong
finish on top of a mediocre middle should not be enough to pull
trend to 1. Quarter-window forces sustained performance.

## Reshape: `R` saturates at a renewable share of 0.5

The first cut took the lifetime renewable share `cum_renewable /
cum_total` as the `R` term directly, linear to 1.0. That implied the
ideal grid was 100% renewable — but scenarios with weather-driven
outages (`scenarios.grid_stress`) make a pure-renewable build worse
than a mixed build with peaker headroom. The score should not push
the agent past the resilience knee.

`R` is now a linear ramp from 0 to `RENEWABLE_TARGET = 0.5`, clamped
to 1.0 above:

    R = min(1.0, renewable_share / RENEWABLE_TARGET)

A 50% renewable mix scores full R credit; going further yields no
marginal score. The raw `renewable_share` is reported alongside `R`
in `components` so the scorecard still surfaces "how green was the
grid" for inspection, without the score implying that 100% is the
target.

These two reshapes land together because the headline-asymptotes-to-
100 promise is what makes the `[0, 100]` axis honest, and the
`R` saturation is what keeps the asymptote reachable in scenarios
where 100% renewable is a losing strategy.
