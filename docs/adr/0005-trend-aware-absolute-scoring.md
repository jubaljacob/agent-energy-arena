# Trend-aware absolute scoring (supersedes the legacy P/T/R formula)

The end-of-game score is a single function: `compute_score(snapshots,
starting_cash)` in `world/scoring.py`. It is **absolute** — score in
`[0, 100]` derived only from the play history and the starting cash
endowment — and **trend-aware** — each of treasury, population, and
happiness contributes a level / trend / trough triple, joined with a
renewable-share term and a solvency term.

A second formula used to live alongside it. `world/scoring.py` (the
legacy module, deleted in this commit) computed a **baseline-relative**
score: `(P/P_ref, tanh(T/T_ref), R)` against `baselines/seed_{N}.json`
generated by a scripted-agent run on the same seed. Its output was a
sub-2.0 weighted sum, not a `[0, 100]` headline. The HTTP `/score`
endpoint was switched to the trend-aware formula earlier;
`evaluate.py::_score_breakdown` and the smoke test continued to call
the legacy form, plus a hand-rolled inline copy in `evaluate.py` for
the `--api-url` mode where no live `World` was available.

Three implementations of "scoring" — two of the legacy formula, one of
the new one — was a [shallow module] cluster. The interface was
not "what is the score?" but "which of the three callsites is asking?"

We retire the legacy form rather than keep it alongside, because:

- **Baseline-relative scoring forces a baseline-keeping discipline.**
  Every seed needs a committed `baselines/seed_{N}.json`. Adding a
  scenario or rebalancing economics invalidates every baseline and
  forces a regeneration sweep.
- **The trend-aware formula already encodes the same intent without
  the baseline.** "Did treasury grow?" is captured by the treasury
  trend term; "did population hold up?" by the population trough;
  "did the agent stay solvent?" by the solvency fraction.
- **One scorer = one place to tune.** Scale anchors
  (`TREASURY_SCALE`, `POP_TARGET`, `CAGR_SCALE_*`, `CVAR_ALPHA`) live
  in `world/scoring.py` only. Recalibration is a one-file change.

The retirement is irreversible by design — `baselines/seed_{N}.json`
files stay on disk for byte-determinism regression tests
(`agents/tests/test_scripted_smoke.py` still pins `(P, T)` against the
committed payload), but no scorer reads them.

## What this means for `evaluate.py`

`cmd_eval` now calls `api.score()` against the same `ApiClient` it
already used for play. `/score` reads per-day snapshots off
`recorder.states_path` and runs `compute_score`. The in-process mode
(`_make_inprocess_client`) and the `--api-url` mode go through the
same HTTP interface, so there is no second implementation to keep in
sync. The printed `score` key changes shape from the legacy
nine-field flat dict `{P, P_ref, p_term, T, T_ref, t_term, R, r_term,
score}` to the trend-aware `{n_days, score, components: {...}}`. Any
external parser of the old shape breaks once at this commit.

## Why this is an ADR, not just a refactor

A future contributor running a "benchmark the agent against a scripted
baseline" experiment could reasonably re-suggest the legacy shape —
the name "baseline-relative scoring" sounds like it should exist.
This ADR records that we considered it, picked absolute trend-aware
instead, and that the deletion was load-bearing for keeping scale
anchors in one place. Reopen the question only if the maintainer
wants to take on the baseline-keeping discipline again.

## Reshape: the headline must asymptote to 100

The first cut of `compute_score` defined the per-axis trend as the
tanh-mapped daily CAGR (or linear slope for treasury). That formula
returns 0.5 for any signal with zero growth — including a signal
parked at its utility ceiling. With happiness clipped at
`HAPPINESS_CEIL=1.2`, a player at the cap had `trend_happy = 0.5` no
matter what, capping `axis_happy` at 0.8 and the achievable headline
at roughly 85. A `/100` headline whose top 15 points are mathematically
unreachable is dishonest signage.

The trend definition is now the **better of growth and the recent
trailing window**:

    trend = max(growth_score, mean(u(xs[-window:])))

with `window = max(1, n // 4)`. Growth keeps doing the work while
the signal is still climbing; once it saturates, the recent-window
utility takes over and credits "stayed at the top." A flat-at-zero
signal still scores trend near zero (recent-window mean is low). A
declining run still scores trend low (both terms low). A
sustained-at-max run scores trend near 1, which is what its time
series deserves.

The quarter-window pick is deliberately not the last-10%: a strong
finish on top of a mediocre middle should not be enough to pull
trend to 1. Quarter-window forces sustained performance.

## Reshape: `R` saturates at a renewable share of 0.5

The first cut took the lifetime renewable share `cum_renewable /
cum_total` as the `R` term directly, linear to 1.0. That implied the
ideal grid was 100% renewable — but scenarios with weather-driven
outages (`scenarios.grid_stress`) make a pure-renewable build worse
than a mixed build with peaker headroom. The score should not push
the agent past the resilience knee.

`R` is now a linear ramp from 0 to `RENEWABLE_TARGET = 0.5`, clamped
to 1.0 above:

    R = min(1.0, renewable_share / RENEWABLE_TARGET)

A 50% renewable mix scores full R credit; going further yields no
marginal score. The raw `renewable_share` is reported alongside `R`
in `components` so the scorecard still surfaces "how green was the
grid" for inspection, without the score implying that 100% is the
target.

These two reshapes land together because the headline-asymptotes-to-
100 promise is what makes the `[0, 100]` axis honest, and the
`R` saturation is what keeps the asymptote reachable in scenarios
where 100% renewable is a losing strategy.

## Treasury anchor fix + recalibration

Two defects made the treasury axis — 30% of the headline — nearly a
constant ~0.50 for any realistic run, so it carried almost no signal:

1. **`starting_cash` was silently ignored.** `compute_score` computed
   `treasury_delta = treasury - starting_cash` but then fed *raw*
   `treasury` to `_u_treasury` for the level and trough terms (the
   delta only reached the slope, where a constant offset cancels). So
   the docstring/ADR promise that "the score measures prosperity
   generated by play, not the size of the endowment" was unimplemented,
   and `--starting-cash` / the `/score` anchor were no-ops — re-scoring
   the same run at 300k / 50k / 5k gave byte-identical output.

2. **The scale was ~16× too large.** `TREASURY_SCALE = 5_000_000` (and
   the matching `13_700/day` trend anchor) was calibrated for an economy
   moving millions. Real runs operate in the 0–300k range, so
   `raw / 5M ≈ 0 → u ≈ 0.5` on every axis term regardless of financial
   performance.

The fix: `_u_treasury` is now called on `treasury_delta` for level and
trough (so `starting_cash` actually anchors the term), and the anchors
drop to `TREASURY_SCALE = 1_000_000` / `TREASURY_TREND_SCALE = 2_740`
(~`SCALE/365`) so the axis has dynamic range over the cash regime real
agents play in. A run that merely preserves its endowment now scores
0.5; one that ends below its endowment (or bleeds into the red late)
scores below 0.5 and the CVaR trough catches the worst days — which,
for a declining run, are the *late* ones.

## Population completion target

`u_pop` was `1 - exp(-p/1000)`: an asymptote that never reaches 1 and
gives only 0.63 at 1000 residents, so a small-but-healthy city
(~150–400 pop) scored a tiny `level_pop` and the population axis stayed
suppressed regardless of play.

`u_pop` is now a **linear ramp to a completion target**, flat above:

    u_pop = min(1.0, max(p, 0) / POP_TARGET),  POP_TARGET = 400

Reaching `POP_TARGET = 400` residents *completes* the population
objective (full credit); growing further yields no marginal score — the
same saturation shape as `R`. This credits realistic city sizes and
makes "did the city reach a self-sustaining population?" a reachable
objective rather than an unreachable asymptote.

## Longevity component

The hackathon rewards agents that survive *and* prosper for ~2 simulated
years within a wall-clock budget, so the score needs a survival term.
Two shapes were on the table:

- **A multiplier** `score = base × L`, with `L` ramping from a floor to
  1.0 at the target horizon. Duration scales the whole score.
- **An additive component** `headline = (1 - w)·base + w·L`, with `L`
  ramping 0→1. Duration adds at most `w·100` points.

We chose **additive**. A multiplier makes duration scale *everything*,
so in a large duration gap a long-but-flat run can out-score a short-but-
thriving one — we measured exactly this on real runs while tuning a
floor-0.4 multiplier (a 414-day flat run beat a 237-day climbing run).
The additive term bounds duration's influence: with `_W_LONGEVITY =
0.15`, longevity moves the headline by at most 15 points and can never
overturn a real trajectory gap. Now that the treasury and population
axes actually measure trajectory (see the two sections above), letting
trajectory dominate is both possible and correct.

    longevity = min(1.0, n_days / target_days)        # target_days = 730
    base      = Σ (the five existing terms, weights summing to 1.0)
    headline  = 100 · ((1 - _W_LONGEVITY)·base + _W_LONGEVITY·longevity)

`longevity` ramps linearly to full credit at `LONGEVITY_TARGET_DAYS =
730` (2 years) and is flat at 1.0 above — extra survival past the target
earns no marginal longevity credit, mirroring how `R` and `u_pop`
saturate. The five base terms are scaled by `(1 - _W_LONGEVITY)` so the
headline stays in `[0, 100]`; this draws the longevity weight
*proportionally* from all five, preserving their relative balance rather
than gutting the two cheap terms. `target_days` is a `compute_score`
parameter (default the constant) so a caller — `evaluate.py
--target-years`, a future `/score?target_years=` — can rescore against a
different horizon without touching the formula.

A consequence worth stating: the perfect-score ceiling now requires
*both* a saturated trajectory and clearing the 2-year horizon. A run
that maxes every axis but stops at 1 year tops out around 92, not 100 —
which is the intended signage for "great, but it didn't go the
distance."

## Recency-weighted treasury trend

`trend_treasury` originally took the linear slope of `treasury_delta`
over the **whole** run. For a run that climbs then collapses, the early
rise and the late fall net out, so the slope — and the trend term —
read ~0.5 (neutral) even as the city bled into insolvency in its final
stretch. The `max(growth, recent_u)` join didn't rescue it either:
`growth` sat at the flat-slope 0.5, which is *above* the declining
recent-level utility, so the `max` kept 0.5.

The fix: `growth_treasury` now reads the slope of the **trailing
window** (`treasury_delta[-window:]`, the same `RECENT_WINDOW_FRACTION`
quarter used by `recent_u`), so "trend" means "where treasury is heading
lately," not "over the whole game." A run still climbing in its last
quarter scores high; a run collapsing in its last quarter scores low.
The `max(growth, recent_u)` structure is unchanged, so a treasury parked
at a surplus (zero recent slope, high recent level) still gets full
trend credit — the ADR's saturated-high case is preserved.

Scope: this applies to the treasury axis only. Population and happiness
use `_trend_cagr` (an end-to-end CAGR), and neither exhibited the
hide-the-collapse failure on the runs reviewed; extending the same
recency treatment to them is a future option, not done here.

A known limit: the window is a quarter, so a collapse confined to the
*final few days* (shorter than the window) is intentionally not enough
to tank the trend — the same "no final-day windfall" symmetry the
quarter-window was chosen for in the saturation reshape above. And a
collapse *from a large surplus* that stays above the endowment for most
of the window still reads near-neutral (the window was mostly healthy);
catching that would need a drawdown term, which is deliberately out of
scope here.
